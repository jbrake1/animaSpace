I. FIELD OF THE INVENTION
The present invention relates to bioreactor systems for culturing mammalian cells in microgravity environments, and more specifically to a rectangular sandwich-stack bioreactor architecture in which each microcarrier bead occupies an individually monitored and individually controlled position between a clean nutrient reservoir and a dirty effluent reservoir, with dedicated piezoelectric micromachined ultrasonic transducer (PMUT) elements at each bead position providing all functions: acoustic streaming to pull fresh medium from the clean reservoir through an inlet hole across the bead surface and push spent medium through an outlet hole into the dirty reservoir; continuous acoustic monitoring of every cell on the bead including detection and counting of every mitosis event, every cell death, and every free-floating cell; viral sentinel detection via resonant mass measurement; and autonomous local control of all bead-level operations without requiring external coordination. Each bead position operates as an independent self-managing bioreactor unit. The aggregate of tens of thousands of such units, stacked in repeating sandwich layers within a rectangular slab, constitutes a cell manufacturing system with exact real-time inventory of every living cell in the system.
The architecture contains almost no mechanical pumps, valves, impellers, filters, or pressure sources. All fluid motion throughout the system is generated by PMUT acoustic streaming. Clean and dirty reservoirs are actively mixed and swept by dedicated agitator and sweeper PMUT elements to maintain uniform concentration throughout each reservoir, eliminating position-dependent nutrient gradients. Volumetric flow balance between the clean and dirty sides is maintained electronically by coordinating the total acoustic streaming across all inlet and outlet PMUTs, ensuring that no net pressure develops on either side of the bead layer. The system is entirely electronic in its fluid control.
The bioreactor supports a provocation quarantine protocol in which chemical agents that force latent viral reactivation are introduced to the clean reservoir, challenging every cell in the system simultaneously while the per-bead through-flow isolates each bead position from its neighbors. Infected cells die in place, their effluent is swept to the dirty reservoir, and viral sentinel sensors confirm the event and cause a cleanse event where the entire bead and inlet and outlet are cavitation cleaned of newly infected cells. The same hardware that performs cleaning continues seamlessly into full production growth moving/accepting healthy cells, changing beads, or reconfiguring any component.
II. BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION
Limitations of Terrestrial Cell Culture
Large-scale mammalian cell culture on Earth is constrained by gravitational sedimentation, mechanical shear from stirring, gas sparging damage, centrifugation losses during harvest, and contamination risk at every mechanical connection point. Typical cell losses of 5 to 15 percent from shear damage during expansion, 5 to 10 percent per centrifugation cycle during harvest, and additional losses from enzymatic detachment collectively degrade both yield and cell quality. Contamination rates of 1 to 3 percent in manufacturing settings are achieved only through massive clean infrastructure investment.
Limitations of Existing Microgravity Cell Culture
Microgravity eliminates sedimentation but also eliminates natural convection, creating stagnant zones where nutrients deplete and waste accumulates. Existing microgravity bioreactor designs import terrestrial solutions—mechanical stirrers, peristaltic pumps, perfusion circuits—importing the same complexity, contamination risk, and shear problems. Gas bubbles generated during culture do not rise to the surface in microgravity, remaining lodged in the medium and blocking flow paths..
The Latent Viral Reactivation Problem in Patient-Derived Cell Culture
A large fraction of the adult population carries latent viral infections including herpes simplex virus, cytomegalovirus, Epstein-Barr virus, human herpesvirus 6, hepatitis B, and human papillomavirus. These viruses persist by integrating into host cell DNA or maintaining episomal genomes, remaining invisible to standard detection methods. The warm, nutrient-rich conditions optimized for cell expansion can trigger latent viral reactivation. In conventional bioreactors with shared medium, virions from a reactivating cell infect neighbors, potentially compromising the entire culture. What is needed is a bioreactor architecture that isolates each cell growth position from its neighbors by physical flow geometry while simultaneously providing the capability to deliberately provoke latent viral reactivation and verify cell-level pathogen clearance.
Limitations of Pressure-Driven Plenum Flow Distribution
Conventional microfluidic devices distribute fluid to parallel channels through pressurized plenums—shared reservoirs where a single pressure source drives flow through multiple outlet holes. Pressure-driven plenums inherently produce non-uniform flow distribution: holes nearest the pressure source receive higher flow than holes at the far end because pressure drops along the plenum length. This gradient worsens with increasing plenum length and increasing number of holes. Equalization requires complex flow resistance matching, tapered plenum geometries, or external flow regulators—all adding mechanical complexity and failing to provide individual per-channel flow control. What is needed is a flow distribution architecture where each channel receives independently controlled flow at rates determined by real-time monitoring of that channel’s biological state, without relying on passive pressure distribution.
Need for a New Architecture
What is needed is a bioreactor architecture designed from first principles for microgravity operation that: provides each cell growth position with its own independently controlled nutrient supply and waste removal driven by acoustic streaming rather than pressure; monitors every cell on every bead position continuously with direct acoustic line-of-sight; maintains exact real-time inventory of every living cell in the system; isolates each bead position from its neighbors by through-flow geometry sufficient to prevent pathogen transmission; provides capability for deliberate provocation quarantine to verify cell-level pathogen clearance; handles physical disruption events gracefully through autonomous local recovery without external intervention; eliminates all mechanical fluid handling components; and scales to arbitrary capacity by extending column length and adding stack layers. The present invention addresses all of these needs.
III. SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION
The present invention provides a rectangular sandwich-stack bioreactor comprising repeating layers, each layer comprising: a clean nutrient reservoir actively mixed by agitator PMUT elements to maintain uniform concentration; a perforated wall with inlet holes at each bead position; a bead layer comprising a planar array of tempered glass beads, each held at a defined position maintained by PMUT dedicated to maintaining the bead position and orientation and surrounded by PMUT elements providing acoustic monitoring from multiple directions, acoustic streaming for individually controlled medium flow, and viral sentinel detection; a perforated wall with outlet holes at each bead position; a dirty effluent reservoir actively swept by sweeper PMUT elements to prevent waste accumulation; and a solid structural wall separating the layer from the next.
At each bead position, inlet PMUT elements pull fresh nutrient-rich medium from the clean reservoir through the inlet hole by acoustic streaming. The medium flows across the bead surface where attached cells consume nutrients and produce metabolic waste. Outlet PMUT elements push the spent medium through the outlet hole into the dirty reservoir by acoustic streaming. The through-flow at each bead position is individually controlled by the local PMUT elements based on real-time monitoring data from that specific bead. All fluid motion is acoustic streaming. Volumetric flow balance between the clean and dirty reservoirs is maintained electronically by coordinating total inlet and outlet streaming across all bead positions, preventing net pressure development on either side.
Each bead position constitutes an autonomous self-managing bioreactor unit. The PMUT elements at each position perform all functions without requiring external coordination: holding the bead in position; pulling fresh medium and pushing spent medium; monitoring every cell on the bead by pulse-echo acoustic sensing with direct line-of-sight from multiple directions; counting every mitosis event and every cell death; detecting free-floating cells in the bead’s vicinity; measuring viral sentinel resonant frequency for pathogen detection; responding to disruption events by relocating dislodged beads and capturing loose cells; and reporting census data including exact living cell count to the system controller. The aggregate of tens of thousands of these autonomous units provides the system controller with an exact real-time inventory of every living cell in the bioreactor at all times.
The through-flow at each bead position provides pathogen isolation between neighboring beads. At operating flow velocities of 50 to 200 micrometers per second, the Péclet number for virus-sized particles (approximately 100 nanometers diameter) exceeds 1,000, meaning convective transport from inlet to outlet dominates lateral diffusion between bead positions by three or more orders of magnitude. A virus released from any bead is swept to the dirty reservoir before it can diffuse to a neighboring bead. This isolation enables a provocation quarantine protocol in which force latent viral reactivation are introduced to the cells, infected cells die in their isolated positions, viral sentinel sensors trigger localized cavitation sterilization of the affected bead position including bead surface, inlet hole, and outlet hole, the sterilized bead is moved to coating chamber and a spare coated bead is placed in the empty position and reseeded with healthy daughter cells from a verified-clean neighbor, all within the same hardware that continues seamlessly.
IV. DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF THE INVENTION
4.1 Sandwich-Stack Architecture
The bioreactor is a rectangular slab comprising repeating sandwich layers stacked in the vertical axis (Z-axis). Each layer contains the following zones, listed from top to bottom in one repeating unit:
Zone A: Clean nutrient reservoir. A shared open volume of approximately 150 to 200 micrometers height spanning the full length (X-axis) and width (Y-axis) of the slab. This reservoir is filled with conditioned, oxygenated culture medium at 37 degrees Celsius during the growth phase. Agitator PMUT elements distributed on the ceiling and floor surfaces of the reservoir generate continuous acoustic streaming currents that keep the medium actively mixed, maintaining uniform nutrient concentration, dissolved oxygen levels, and pH throughout the reservoir volume. The mixing prevents any position-dependent gradients that would cause some beads to receive richer medium than others. The reservoir connects to the manifold clean medium supply through inlet ports at the slab edges.
Zone B: Inlet perforated wall. A solid structural wall of approximately 50 to 100 micrometers thickness fabricated from silicon, glass, or ceramic, with an array of through-holes at each bead position. Each through-hole has associated PMUT elements on its inner surface that generate acoustic streaming to pull medium from the clean reservoir through the hole and into the bead layer below. The PMUT elements at each hole are individually addressable and independently controllable. The hole diameter is approximately 50 to 100 micrometers, sized to permit adequate flow while preventing bead passage.
Zone C: Bead layer. An open planar space of approximately 200 to 300 micrometers height in which the microcarrier beads are held at defined positions. Each bead is held and oriented between the inlet and outlet by dedicated PMUTs. The bead’s attached cells face the open space between the inlet wall above and the outlet wall below. Fresh medium entering from the inlet hole above flows across the bead and its attached cells, delivering nutrients and oxygen and carrying away metabolic waste. PMUT elements on the inlet wall, outlet wall, and side surfaces of the bead layer provide acoustic monitoring of the bead and its cells from multiple directions with direct unobstructed line-of-sight. The bead layer is an open plane—a vast field—where each bead sits at its designated position with open space surrounding it on all sides, when combined with flow provides a powerful quarantine method.
Zone D: Outlet perforated wall. A solid structural wall with through-holes at each bead position, mirroring the inlet wall. PMUT elements at each outlet hole generate acoustic streaming to push spent medium from the bead layer into the dirty reservoir below. The outlet PMUTs at each bead position are independently controllable.
Zone E: Dirty effluent reservoir. A shared open volume of approximately 150 to 200 micrometers height collecting spent medium from all bead positions in the layer. Sweeper PMUT elements on the reservoir surfaces generate acoustic streaming that continuously moves effluent toward the outlet ports at the slab edges, preventing waste accumulation or stagnation anywhere in the reservoir. The reservoir connects to the manifold dirty medium return through outlet ports at the slab edges.
Zone F: Solid structural wall. A continuous wall of approximately 50 to 100 micrometers thickness separating this layer from the next, providing structural integrity and complete physical isolation between adjacent layers.
Total stack height per repeating unit is approximately 650 to 900 micrometers. The number of layers is determined by the slab thickness. The column length (number of bead positions per row) is determined by the slab length in the X-axis. The number of parallel columns is determined by the slab width in the Y-axis. All three dimensions scale independently, enabling the bioreactor capacity to be configured for any cell production requirement.
4.2 Bead Positions and Capacity
Bead positions are arrayed on a regular grid within each bead layer at a pitch of approximately 250 to 400 micrometers in both the X-axis and Y-axis. At a pitch of 300 micrometers, a slab of 10 centimeters length and 10 centimeters width accommodates approximately 333 bead positions per row and approximately 333 rows per layer, yielding approximately 110,000 bead positions per layer. At a layer stack height of approximately 800 micrometers, a slab of 5 centimeters thickness accommodates approximately 62 layers, yielding approximately 6.8 million bead positions per slab. In practice, the slab dimensions and bead pitch are selected based on the cell production requirement, the available manifold volume, and the thermal management capacity of the system. A slab the size of a credit card with modest thickness can accommodate hundreds of thousands to millions of bead positions, each individually monitored and controlled.
At confluence, each bead of 100 to 200 micrometers diameter supports approximately 70 to 210 cells depending on cell type. A slab with 100,000 bead positions at confluence produces approximately 7 to 21 million cells. A slab with 1 million bead positions produces 70 to 210 million cells. Cell yield scales linearly with bead count.
4.3 PMUT Bead Manipulation and Monitoring
Dedicated bead manipulation PMUT arrays maintain each bead within a precise three-dimensional position between the inlet and outlet PMUT arrays. These elements are specifically tuned to the acoustic impedance of chemically tempered glass beads, operating at optimal frequency and power levels so that the minimum energy required is used to hold the bead at its designated location. The manipulation PMUTs can orient the bead to expose different surface regions to the through-flow, or continuously rotate the bead if the resulting mechanical stimulation promotes cell growth and survivability. The same arrays provide constant acoustic monitoring of each bead's cell population, with sensing parameters optimized for the impedance contrast between tempered glass and living cells, enabling precise detection of cell count, distribution, viability, and attachment quality across the bead surface.
4.4 Clean Reservoir as Mass Migration Layer
During normal growth operations, the clean reservoir serves exclusively as a nutrient supply volume. However, at the beginning of a new growth cycle, the clean reservoir functions as a mass migration layer for cell delivery. The reservoir is a wide open space with no obstructions and an inlet hole at every bead position, making it ideal for rapid and uniform cell seeding. Cells suspended in conditioned medium are distributed across the reservoir by agitator PMUTs and pulled to their assigned bead positions through the inlet holes, ensuring the system reaches full seeding with no wasted time. At harvest, the bead layer transitions from growth mode to release mode. Temperature is reduced from 37 degrees Celsius to approximately 15 to 20 degrees Celsius over several minutes, progressively weakening integrin-mediated cell adhesion by slowing actin polymerization, reducing focal adhesion kinase activity, and decreasing membrane fluidity. As adhesion strength degrades, PMUT elements at each bead position activate in a detachment mode generating acoustic streaming, acoustic radiation pressure, and localized substrate vibration directed to dislodge cells from the bead surface. Perfusion flow rate is simultaneously increased to carry released cells away from the bead and through the inlet holes into the clean reservoir, where agitator PMUTs route them to the harvest collection port.
Bead positions that were flagged during growth-phase monitoring for anomalous behavior including unexplained cell deaths, unusual acoustic signatures, or sentinel alerts have their cells routed through the outlet holes into the dirty reservoir for disposal rather than through the inlet to harvest. This routing decision is made per-bead based on growth-phase monitoring history, not by individual cell inspection at the moment of harvest. Cells that do not release under the physical detachment conditions are flushed to the dirty reservoir as waste. The residual fraction is typically 5 percent or less and is expected to be enriched in cells with abnormal adhesion phenotypes including senescent cells and cells with overly mature focal adhesions, making their exclusion a net quality improvement to the harvest population. Once in the clean reservoir, harvested cells are routed to the manifold for downstream processing.


4.5 Per-Bead Autonomous PMUT Control Unit
Each bead position in the bioreactor is served by a dedicated set of PMUT elements constituting an autonomous control unit. The PMUT elements at each position are fabricated on the wall substrates surrounding the bead’s location in the bead layer: on the inlet perforated wall above, the outlet perforated wall below. Each control unit performs all functions for its bead position independently, without requiring instructions or coordination from neighboring control units or from a centralized controller, except for reporting census data and receiving system-level commands such as provocation initiation or harvest.
The functions performed by each per-bead autonomous control unit are as follows:
Bead holding assistance. The PMUT elements can generate sufficient acoustic radiation pressure to center the bead precisely on its 3D position and to reposition as needed.
Inlet flow control. The inlet PMUT elements generate acoustic streaming that pulls fresh medium from the clean reservoir through the inlet hole and across the bead surface. The flow rate is continuously adjusted based on the monitored metabolic state of the cells on the bead. A bead with many actively dividing cells receives higher flow. A bead with a single newly attached cell receives minimal flow. The flow rate decision is made locally by the control unit based on its own monitoring data.

Cell detachment for harvest. During harvest operations, the PMUT elements at each bead position operate in a detachment mode distinct from normal growth-phase functions. The inlet and outlet PMUT elements generate acoustic streaming patterns directed along the bead surface to create shear forces against cells whose adhesion has been weakened by temperature reduction. Bead-facing PMUT elements generate acoustic radiation pressure pushing cells away from the bead surface. Where bead geometry and PMUT positioning permit, elements drive resonant vibration modes in the bead substrate itself, producing oscillatory acceleration at the bead surface that exceeds the weakened adhesion forces. The detachment mode operates at higher power than growth-phase monitoring and flow control but well below the cavitation power levels used for sterilization. The control unit monitors cell release acoustically during detachment and reports harvest yield and residual cell count to the system controller.
Outlet flow control. The outlet PMUT elements generate acoustic streaming that pushes spent medium from the bead surface through the outlet hole into the dirty reservoir. The outlet flow rate is coordinated with the inlet flow rate to maintain zero net pressure. This coordination is local—each control unit balances its own inlet and outlet.
Acoustic monitoring. PMUT elements surrounding the bead perform pulse-echo acoustic sensing during periodic census bursts. Each element has direct unobstructed line-of-sight to the bead and its attached cells. The monitoring provides: bead presence verification at the expected position; cell count on the bead surface determined by acoustic signature mapping of the bead’s cell-covered and bare regions; detection of every mitosis event by observing a single cell signature splitting into two closely spaced signatures; detection of every cell death by characteristic acoustic signature changes including swelling, membrane integrity loss, and eventual lysis; detection of free-floating cells in the open space around the bead; detection of debris; and measurement of medium acoustic properties indicating local nutrient and waste levels.
Viral sentinel monitoring. One or more PMUT elements at each bead position, preferentially on the outlet side directly in the spent medium flow path, are functionalized as resonant mass viral sentinel sensors. These elements carry virus-binding functionalization (lectins, heparan sulfate mimetics, sialic acid derivatives, or antibody cocktails) on their membrane surfaces and perform resonant frequency self-measurement during each census burst. Virus particles binding to the functionalized surface add mass, shifting the resonant frequency downward. Because each sentinel sees the concentrated effluent from a single bead rather than diluted bulk medium, the sensitivity is maximized. A single lytic viral event at one bead position is detectable by that position’s sentinel within one to several census cycles.
Census reporting. Each control unit reports its census data to the system controller after each burst: bead present/absent, cell count, cells gained since last census (mitosis events), cells lost since last census (deaths), free-floating cells detected, sentinel frequency shift, and any anomalies. The system controller aggregates these reports into a real-time inventory of every living cell in the bioreactor. The inventory is exact, not estimated, because every cell on every bead is individually monitored.
Cytopathic effect detection. The acoustic monitoring at each bead position tracks the temporal evolution of each cell’s acoustic signature. Viral cytopathic effect produces a characteristic pattern: gradual cell swelling with preserved membrane integrity followed by abrupt lysis. This pattern is distinguishable from apoptosis (shrinkage then fragmentation) and necrosis (rapid swelling with early membrane permeability). Detection of this pattern at any bead position triggers a viral alert complementing the sentinel data.
4.6 Autonomous Disruption Recovery
Physical disruption of the bioreactor—occurring during transport, docking, undocking, manifold reconfiguration, or any mechanical event that subjects the slab to acceleration or vibration—is an expected operational event, not a failure state. During a disruption, beads may dislodge from their holding positions, cells may detach from beads, and the spatial organization of the bead layer may be partially or completely disordered.
Recovery is autonomous and requires no external intervention. Immediately following a disruption, every per-bead control unit executes a recovery protocol:
Step 1: Bead location check. Each control unit performs a pulse-echo scan to determine whether its bead is present at the expected position. If the bead is present, proceed to Step 3. If absent, proceed to Step 2.
Step 2: Bead relocation. The control unit, in coordination with neighboring control units, performs an acoustic search of the local bead layer volume to locate dislodged beads. Once located, PMUT elements generate acoustic radiation pressure to push the beads back toward their assigned location. The relocation uses slightly higher acoustic force than normal operations to overcome the higher inertia and drag of the beads.
Step 3: Cell census. With the bead in position, the control unit performs a detailed acoustic census of the bead surface, counting surviving attached cells and assessing their viability. The pre-disruption cell count is known from the last census report. The difference between pre-disruption and post-disruption counts is the disruption cell loss for that bead position.
Step 4: Dead cell flush. Any dead or dying cells detected on the bead are removed by outlet flow. The control unit increases outlet streaming briefly to flush debris into the effluent layer and or uses acoustic radiation pressure to push the cells to the outlet.
Step 5: Loose cell management. Healthy or unhealthy free-floating cells detected in the bead layer space—cells that detached from beads are captured by the nearest control unit’s acoustic manipulation and either returned to a bead with available surface area or, if non-viable, flushed to the effluent layer.
Step 6: Status report. Each control unit reports its post-recovery status: bead in position (yes/no), current cell count, cells lost, cells captured from neighbors, and any anomalies. The system controller aggregates these reports into a post-disruption inventory showing exact cell losses and the new baseline for continued growth monitoring.
The recovery process completes in seconds to minutes depending on disruption severity. The bioreactor returns to normal growth operation with updated cell counts. Disruption events are logged with cell loss data for build planning purposes. The system controller may adjust growth timeline estimates based on cumulative disruption losses.
4.7 Electronic Flow Balance and Reservoir Management
The bioreactor contains very few pumps, valves, or pressure sources. All fluid motion is generated by PMUT acoustic streaming. This requires active electronic management to maintain proper flow balance between the clean and dirty sides of each bead layer.
At each bead position, the inlet PMUT pulls a volume of fresh medium from the clean reservoir and the outlet PMUT pushes an equal volume of spent medium into the dirty reservoir. The local control unit balances its own inlet and outlet flow rates. Across the full bead layer, the total volumetric flow pulled from the clean reservoir equals the total volumetric flow pushed to the dirty reservoir. This balance is maintained by the system controller, which monitors the aggregate flow from all control unit reports and issues minor adjustments if cumulative drift is detected.
If the total inlet flow were to exceed the total outlet flow, medium would accumulate in the bead layer, eventually creating positive pressure that could drive uncontrolled flow through the outlet holes. Conversely, if outlet exceeds inlet, the bead layer would deplete, potentially drawing uncontrolled flow from the clean reservoir. Either imbalance would bypass the per-bead PMUT flow control. The electronic coordination prevents this by enforcing volumetric balance at all times.
The clean reservoir is actively mixed by agitator PMUT elements that generate continuous circulating currents throughout the reservoir volume. This mixing ensures that the medium at every inlet hole position has identical nutrient concentration, dissolved oxygen, pH, and temperature regardless of the hole’s distance from the manifold supply port. Without active mixing, nutrient depletion near high-demand beads and nutrient concentration near the supply port would create gradients that starve some beads and overfeed others. The agitator PMUTs eliminate this problem by maintaining uniform conditions everywhere in the reservoir.
The dirty reservoir is actively swept by sweeper PMUT elements that generate continuous flow toward the manifold outlet ports. This sweeping prevents waste accumulation or stagnation in the dirty reservoir, ensuring that high concentrations of metabolic waste, dead cell debris, and any pathogens flushed from bead positions are rapidly transported out of the slab to the reconditioning module. The sweeping also prevents backpressure buildup in the dirty reservoir that could oppose the outlet PMUTs at individual bead positions.
The manifold reconditioning module receives spent medium from the dirty reservoir, processes it through acoustic debris filtration, nanofiltration at approximately 20 nanometer pore size for virion removal, ultraviolet-C irradiation for pathogen inactivation, chemical reconditioning, gas exchange, and nutrient replenishment, and returns the reconditioned medium to the clean reservoir. The reconditioning loop operates continuously, maintaining the clean reservoir at specification.
4.8 Provocation Quarantine Protocol
The per-bead through-flow isolation provided by the sandwich-stack architecture enables a provocation quarantine protocol that deliberately forces latent viral reactivation to verify cell-level pathogen clearance. The quarantine is performed in the same bioreactor hardware that will subsequently be used for production growth, with no cell transfer or vessel change required.
Pre-launch screening. Before patient cells are cryopreserved and launched to orbit, they are screened on Earth using polymerase chain reaction (PCR) panels targeting known viral families, next-generation metagenomic sequencing to detect unknown or unexpected viral sequences, and digital droplet PCR (ddPCR) for absolute quantification of viral genome copies per cell. This screening characterizes the patient’s viral profile and determines which provocation agents and sentinel functionalizations are appropriate.
Phase 1: Seeding (hours 0 to 6). Patient cells, thawed from cryopreservation, are introduced to the bioreactor and distributed to bead positions. Each per-bead control unit receives and monitors its cell. Standard attachment and spreading over 6 hours.
Phase 2: Provocation (hours 6 to 54). Provocation agents are introduced to the clean reservoir and mixed uniformly by the agitator PMUTs. The provocation cocktail is tailored to the patient’s known viral profile and includes one or more of: sodium butyrate (a histone deacetylase inhibitor that triggers reactivation of Epstein-Barr virus, Kaposi’s sarcoma-associated herpesvirus, and other herpesviruses); 5-azacytidine (a demethylating agent that reactivates silenced proviral genomes across multiple virus families by removing the methyl groups that suppress viral gene expression); 12-O-tetradecanoylphorbol-13-acetate (TPA, a phorbol ester that activates latent retroviruses and herpesviruses); valproic acid (an alternative histone deacetylase inhibitor used in HIV shock-and-kill research). The agents are used at concentrations validated to provoke viral reactivation without killing uninfected cells.
During provocation, any cell carrying a latent virus that responds to the agents begins viral gene expression. The infected cell produces virions and eventually lyses. The virions are swept from the bead position to the dirty reservoir by the per-bead through-flow, which provides isolation with a Péclet number exceeding 1,000 for virus-sized particles. The viral sentinel PMUT on the outlet side of the affected bead position detects the virion mass loading by resonant frequency shift.The per-bead control unit detects the cell death by acoustic signature change. The control unit then initiates a localized cleanse event: the inlet and outlet PMUTs at the affected bead position operate at maximum power to generate focused cavitation sterilizing the bead surface, inlet hole walls, and outlet hole walls, destroying any residual virions, viral protein, or cell debris. The now very clean bead needs to be replaced by a bead that is clean and has the proper coating. A spare pre-preped bead is moved into position using the whisper tech the clean bead is moved to the used beads storage.  
Phase 3: Verification (hours 54 to 72). Provocation agents are flushed from the clean reservoir and replaced with standard culture medium. A monitoring window of 12 to 18 hours allows detection of late reactivators. At the end of the window, a comprehensive verification survey is performed: every viral sentinel in the system is interrogated for any frequency shift above baseline; every per-bead control unit reports cell count and viability; the system controller compiles a complete map showing which bead positions had viral events (infected cells that died) and which had clean cells that survived provocation without producing detectable virus.
Phase 4: Seamless transition to production. The bead positions containing verified clean surviving cells continue directly into production growth. No cells are moved. No hardware is changed. The per-bead control units resume normal growth monitoring. Medium exchange rates increase as cells divide and metabolic demand rises. The provocation quarantine is complete and the bioreactor is now operating as a production system with verified-clean cells.
Bead positions where infected cells died during provocation are empty. As the surviving clean cells divide and produce daughter cells, the per-bead control units at neighboring positions detect free-floating daughter cells and guide them to the empty bead positions, repopulating the vacated plots with verified-clean progeny. Over the course of the expansion phase, the entire bead population reaches confluence with clean cells.
The provocation quarantine provides pathogen clearance that is active rather than passive—latent viruses are forced to reveal themselves rather than hoping they stay dormant. Clearance is verified at the individual bead level by per-bead sentinel sensors seeing concentrated single-bead effluent. The combination of deliberate provocation, per-bead flow isolation, per-bead sentinel detection, and comprehensive post-provocation verification provides a pathogen management capability that no prior art bioreactor system offers.
4.9 Cell Loading, Seeding, and Daughter Cell Management
Cells are introduced to the bioreactor through the clean reservoir from the manifold supply port. The cells enter the reservoir suspended in conditioned medium. PMUTs distribute the cells throughout the reservoir. When a cell enters a bead position, the control unit detects it acoustically and reduces inlet and outlet flow and then acoustically moves the cell onto the bead surface and let it attach. 
During growth, cells divide on the bead surface. Each mitosis event is detected by the local control unit as a single acoustic cell signature splitting into two closely spaced signatures. The daughter cell may remain attached to the same bead or may detach and float in the bead layer space. If detached, the nearest control unit with available bead surface area captures the daughter cell using gentle acoustic streaming and guides it to the bead. This management is local—each control unit knows its own cell count and available capacity, and control units at adjacent positions can coordinate to direct a daughter cell to the nearest bead that needs one.
As cells grow toward confluence, the system controller has exact knowledge of every bead’s cell count and every cell’s history. This data feeds the build controller that coordinates harvest timing across all bioreactors in the manufacturing system.
4.10 Harvest by Physical Detachment
At the conclusion of the growth phase, the bioreactor transitions to harvest using a purely physical detachment method requiring no enzymes, chemical agents, or specialized surface coatings. The method exploits the fundamental biology of cell adhesion: integrin-mediated focal adhesions are actively maintained structures requiring continuous ATP-dependent actin polymerization, focal adhesion kinase activity, and myosin-II contractility. Reducing temperature throttles these metabolic processes, progressively degrading adhesion strength without harming cell viability.
The harvest sequence proceeds as follows. The bioreactor temperature is reduced from 37 degrees Celsius to approximately 15 to 20 degrees Celsius over a period of several minutes. The rate of cooling is controlled to avoid thermal shock while achieving sufficient adhesion weakening. As temperature decreases, actin polymerization rate constants drop, focal adhesion turnover slows, membrane fluidity decreases reducing integrin lateral mobility and clustering, and myosin-II contractility falls. The cumulative effect is a 50 to 70 percent reduction in cell adhesion force, from the typical 10 to 100 nanonewtons per cell range down to levels where moderate physical forces achieve detachment.
With adhesion weakened, the per-bead PMUT elements activate in detachment mode. Three acoustic mechanisms contribute to cell release. First, acoustic streaming generates directed fluid flow along the bead surface, creating shear stress in the 1 to 10 dynes per square centimeter range that exceeds the now-reduced adhesion forces. Second, acoustic radiation pressure from bead-facing PMUT elements pushes directly on cells, adding a normal force component to the tangential shear. Third, where geometry permits, PMUT elements drive resonant vibration in the bead substrate, producing oscillatory surface acceleration that mechanically disrupts remaining adhesion contacts. The combination of these three mechanisms with the reduced-temperature adhesion weakening achieves detachment of approximately 95 percent or more of the cell population.
Simultaneously, perfusion flow rate is increased to carry detached cells away from the bead surface. Cells released from beads at positions with clean growth-phase history and still pass simple impedance test to insure it’s still healthy  are routed through the inlet hole into the clean reservoir and swept to the harvest collection port. Cells released from bead positions flagged during growth-phase monitoring for anomalous events including unexplained deaths, atypical acoustic signatures, or other alerts are routed through the outlet holes into the dirty reservoir for disposal. This per-bead routing decision is based on the complete growth-phase monitoring record for each bead position, providing quality control at harvest without requiring real-time deep individual cell inspection.
Cells that do not release under the combined temperature-reduction and acoustic detachment conditions are not pursued with additional chemical or enzymatic treatment. Instead, increased flow and acoustic energy sufficient to cause cavitation will flush the residual fraction to the dirty reservoir as waste. This residual population, typically 5 percent or less of the bead population, is expected to be enriched in cells exhibiting abnormal adhesion phenotypes including senescent cells with heavily remodeled extracellular matrix, cells with overly mature focal adhesion complexes, and cells that have undergone partial epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition. Discarding these cells rather than adding process complexity to recover them provides a net improvement in harvest quality while reducing system complexity, eliminating enzymatic reagent supply chains, and avoiding regulatory questions associated with residual protease activity in the cell product.
In an alternative embodiment, the glass microcarrier beads carry a thermoresponsive polymer coating such as poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) (PNIPAAm) beneath the extracellular matrix protein layer, with a lower critical solution temperature transition at approximately 32 degrees Celsius. In this embodiment, cells detach as the coating transitions from hydrophobic to hydrophilic during cooling, and the acoustic detachment mode serves as a secondary mechanism for any cells not released by the coating transition alone. The thermoresponsive coating embodiment may achieve higher initial release percentages but adds coating complexity and a supply chain dependency that the primary physical detachment method avoids.
The beads remain held at their positions by the bead-holding PMUT elements throughout harvest. Because every cell was individually monitored throughout the growth phase, the system controller knows before harvest exactly how many living cells are on each bead and their health status. The harvest cell count is predicted before detachment begins and verified as cells are collected at the harvest port. The residual cell count remaining on each bead after detachment is measured by post-harvest acoustic census. If the residual fraction at any bead position exceeds expected levels, this data is logged for process health monitoring. A progressive increase in the residual fraction across successive harvest cycles may indicate surface fouling, coating degradation, or biofilm formation on bead surfaces, providing an early diagnostic of process drift at no additional hardware cost.
4.11 Zero-Consumable Multi-Modal Sterilization
Between build cycles, the bioreactor undergoes sterilization. The sterilization sequence comprises: cavitation sterilization by operating all PMUT elements at maximum power with phased-array focusing to generate scanning cavitation throughout the bead layer, reservoir volumes, and all inlet and outlet holes; thermal enhancement at sustained maximum power raising temperature to at least 121 degrees Celsius; drainage and exposure to orbital hard vacuum; ultraviolet-C irradiation at 265 nanometers; acoustic census verification of sterility; and ECM protein re-coating and sentinel re-functionalization. Duration: approximately 2 to 4 hours. Consumables: zero cleaning agents.
4.12 Manufacturing Vessel Radiation Shielding
4.12.1 Radiation as a Fundamental Threat to Organ Manufacturing in Orbit
The bioreactor system described in this invention produces cells destined for assembly into viable transplantable organs. The entire manufacturing process—cell expansion, provocation quarantine, production growth, harvest, and tissue assembly—depends on maintaining genetic integrity across billions of cell divisions. Any source of unrepaired DNA damage introduces mutations that propagate to all daughter cells and persist in the finished organ. Radiation-induced genetic damage is therefore not a secondary engineering concern but a fundamental threat to the viability of the manufactured product.
In low Earth orbit outside dedicated shielding, the ambient radiation environment is approximately 0.5 to 1 millisievert per day, which is 150 to 365 times the Earth surface background rate of approximately 6.6 microsieverts per day. The radiation field comprises three components: trapped proton and electron belts, solar particle events, and galactic cosmic rays. Of these, galactic cosmic rays present the greatest biological hazard because they include high-energy heavy ions such as iron, carbon, and oxygen nuclei that deposit massive energy along narrow tracks through biological tissue. A single iron ion traversing a cell nucleus deposits sufficient energy to cause dozens of double-strand DNA breaks in a tight spatial cluster. These clustered lesions are qualitatively different from the isolated single-strand breaks caused by terrestrial background radiation and are largely unrepairable by any known biological DNA repair mechanism.
4.12.2 Mitotic Radiosensitivity and Organ Manufacturing
The bioreactor system is designed to expand cell populations through rapid division. During active production growth, a significant fraction of the cell population is in mitosis at any given moment. Cells in mitosis are acutely vulnerable to radiation damage because the nuclear envelope has dissolved, chromosomes are condensed and maximally exposed on the metaphase plate, and the homologous recombination and non-homologous end joining repair pathways are largely suppressed during the mitotic phase of the cell cycle. A double-strand break sustained during mitosis cannot be repaired before chromosome segregation and is either mis-segregated, producing daughter cells with incorrect chromosome counts, or mis-repaired after division, producing translocations, deletions, or other structural chromosome abnormalities.
In an unshielded or inadequately shielded orbital environment, the cumulative effect across a production run of days to weeks is not merely the loss of individual damaged cells but the progressive introduction of mutations into the surviving and dividing population. Damaged cells that survive and continue dividing pass their mutations to all progeny. Because the bioreactor system produces cells for assembly into complex vascularized organs comprising multiple cooperating cell types, genetic damage in any one cell lineage can compromise the structural integrity, metabolic function, immune compatibility, or long-term safety of the finished organ. A transplantable organ manufactured from cells with elevated mutation burden presents unacceptable risks including impaired tissue function, immune rejection triggered by neoantigen expression from mutated genes, and increased long-term cancer risk in the transplant recipient.
The per-bead acoustic monitoring system described in this invention detects cell death, abnormal morphology, and gross cytopathic effects but cannot detect point mutations, small deletions, or chromosomal translocations at the single-cell level during production. Radiation-induced genetic damage is therefore a silent failure mode that acoustic monitoring alone cannot catch. The only reliable mitigation is to prevent the damage from occurring by reducing the radiation environment to levels at which the biological DNA repair machinery can maintain genetic integrity at rates equivalent to or better than terrestrial cell culture conditions.
4.12.3 Electronic Vulnerability
The bioreactor architecture depends on tens of thousands of per-bead autonomous PMUT control units making real-time decisions about cell management, flow control, acoustic monitoring, viral sentinel detection, and harvest routing. Each control unit contains digital logic and memory elements that are susceptible to single-event effects caused by energetic charged particles traversing semiconductor junctions. Single-event upsets flip stored bits in memory and registers, potentially causing a control unit to misidentify cell states, miscalculate flow rates, miss viral sentinel alerts, or issue incorrect routing commands during harvest. Single-event latchups can cause destructive overcurrent conditions that permanently damage circuit elements. Single-event functional interrupts can halt processor operation requiring reset and loss of monitoring continuity.
Radiation-hardened electronics designed to withstand orbital radiation environments exist but are typically two or more technology generations behind commercial silicon in processing speed, power efficiency, and integration density, and cost 10 to 100 times more per unit than commercial equivalents. The per-bead control architecture described in this invention requires high integration density and low power per unit to be economically and thermally viable at the scale of tens of thousands to millions of control units per bioreactor slab. By shielding the entire manufacturing vessel to below Earth surface background radiation levels, all electronic components operate in a radiation environment equivalent to or gentler than a terrestrial data center, enabling the use of commercial-grade semiconductor components without radiation hardening. This provides access to the latest available silicon technology at commercial pricing, a significant advantage in system performance, power efficiency, and manufacturing cost.
4.12.4 Hull Design and Shielding Physics
Radiation shielding for galactic cosmic rays and trapped belt particles operates by fundamentally different physics than shielding for photon radiation such as X-rays or gamma rays. Energetic charged particles lose energy primarily through electromagnetic interactions with atomic electrons in the shielding material. The energy loss per unit mass of shielding, known as stopping power, is highest in materials with the highest ratio of atomic electrons to nucleon mass, which means hydrogen-rich materials provide the most shielding per unit mass. Hydrogen has one electron per one nucleon mass unit, the highest ratio of any element. Iron, by comparison, has 26 electrons per 56 nucleon mass units. Per kilogram of shielding launched to orbit, hydrogen-rich materials stop approximately twice as many galactic cosmic ray particles as metallic shielding.
Additionally, when energetic heavy ions strike high-atomic-number nuclei in metallic shielding, the target nucleus fragments in a process called spallation, producing a shower of secondary particles including neutrons, protons, and lighter ions that can be more biologically damaging than the original incident particle. Hydrogen nuclei are too light to produce significant spallation cascades. A proton-on-proton collision either scatters or stops the incident particle without generating a secondary shower. Hydrogen-rich shielding therefore provides superior stopping power per unit mass and simultaneously avoids the secondary particle production that degrades the effectiveness of metallic shielding.
The manufacturing vessel hull comprises, from exterior to interior: an outer stainless steel pressure hull providing structural integrity and micrometeorite protection; thermal insulation; a polyethylene structural layer of at least 20 centimeters providing hydrogen-rich particle shielding with structural contribution; a self-healing paraffin wax fill layer providing additional hydrogen-rich shielding with the property that micrometeorite punctures through the wax layer tend to reseal as the wax flows to fill the puncture channel; a sealed water tank layer of at least 20 centimeters providing hydrogen-rich shielding that also serves as thermal mass for temperature stability; and an inner stainless steel pressure hull. The stainless steel layers are kept as thin as structural requirements permit to minimize spallation secondary production. The combined hydrogen-rich shielding layers provide a total areal density of at least 40 grams per square centimeter.
At this areal density of hydrogen-rich material, the galactic cosmic ray dose rate inside the vessel is reduced to below the Earth surface background rate of approximately 2.4 millisieverts per year. The manufactured cells and assembled organs therefore experience a radiation environment that is equivalent to or better than any terrestrial cell culture facility or organ manufacturing clean room. This enables the legitimate regulatory claim that orbital manufacturing introduces no additional radiation-induced mutation burden compared to approved terrestrial cell manufacturing processes.
4.12.5 Construction and Filling Sequence
The manufacturing vessel is constructed in orbit by a multi-launch assembly sequence. The structural hull, thermal insulation, and polyethylene layers are launched as dry structural components and assembled in orbit. The water tank layer and paraffin wax fill layer are filled after the vessel is in its operational orbit, because launching the shielding mass as liquid or solid fill in the same vehicle as the structure would unnecessarily constrain launch vehicle payload capacity. Water and wax are delivered by dedicated resupply launches and pumped or injected into their respective hull compartments. The bioreactor slabs, electronic systems, and all other internal equipment are installed after the shielding hull is complete and verified, ensuring that no sensitive components are exposed to the unshielded orbital radiation environment at any point during assembly.
4.13 Medium Reconditioning Pathogen Barrier
The manifold reconditioning module provides an independent pathogen barrier. All spent medium from the dirty reservoir passes through: nanofiltration at approximately 20 nanometer pore size removing virions; UV-C irradiation inactivating survivors; acoustic debris filtration; chemical reconditioning; gas exchange; and nutrient replenishment. Optionally, antiviral agents tailored to the patient’s viral profile are maintained at prophylactic concentrations. The reconditioning barrier prevents any pathogen from cycling back to the clean reservoir, complementing the per-bead through-flow isolation within the bioreactor.
4.14 Anti-Fouling and Selective Coating
PMUT surfaces, magnetic feature surfaces, structural wall surfaces, and non-functionalized sentinel surfaces carry anti-fouling treatments (polyethylene glycol silanes, fluorinated silanes, zwitterionic coatings, diamond-like carbon, or combinations) reducing protein adsorption by at least 80 percent. Bead surfaces do not carry anti-fouling treatment, enabling selective ECM protein adsorption during the re-coating phase. Treatment thickness of 1 to 500 nanometers has no effect on acoustic or magnetic performance.
4.15 Remote Teleoperated Maintenance
Hardware maintenance is performed by teleoperated robotic manipulators with redundant units capable of repairing other manipulators. Tasks include slab repositioning, docking operations, bead reloading, part replacement, and component inspection.
4.16 Bead Coating Chamber 
Following cavitation sterilization at their bead layer positions, beads are transferred to a dedicated coating chamber for extracellular matrix protein application. The coating chamber is a cylinder with tapered ends, sized so that the full bead population from a bioreactor slab packs tightly and fills the chamber volume completely. Coating fluid containing the appropriate ECM protein is flushed through one of the tapered ends and pushed through the packed bead stack, ensuring that every bead surface is thoroughly and uniformly coated. Following coating, the chamber is flushed with clean fluid to remove all residual coating fluid, which is recovered and recycled. The coated beads are then returned to their bead layer positions for the next build cycle. The coating chamber is isolated from the bioreactor fluid system by mechanical valves that prevent any mixing of coating fluids with the normal bioreactor culture medium. The coating chamber is the only component in the bioreactor system that employs mechanical valves; all other fluid control throughout the system is performed by PMUT acoustic streaming.
4.17 Positional Identity Conditioning System


The bioreactor architecture provides a capability beyond cell growth and pathogen clearance: the deliberate conditioning of each cell's positional identity before harvest, such that cells arrive at the assembly chamber already carrying the epigenetic state appropriate to their intended anatomical location within the target tissue construct.
Within any complex organ, cells of the same nominal type occupy distinct sub-regional identities with different gene expression profiles, metabolic functions, and physiological roles. In the liver, periportal hepatocytes (zone 1) and centrilobular hepatocytes (zone 3) are both hepatocytes, but they are not interchangeable. Their positional identity is encoded epigenetically through chromatin state, DNA methylation patterns, and sustained transcription factor networks. Cells grown under standard culture conditions acquire a positional epigenetic state determined by their culture environment, which typically does not match the specific sub-regional identity required in the assembled organ.
The positional identity conditioning system addresses this by delivering organ-position-specific morphogen and signaling factor cocktails to designated layers of the bioreactor during a conditioning phase that precedes harvest. Because each layer of the sandwich-stack has an independent clean nutrient reservoir, distinct medium compositions can be delivered to distinct layers simultaneously without any modification to the bead layer, the PMUT array, or the per-bead control architecture. The conditioning capability is entirely a function of fluid composition delivered through the existing manifold connection to each layer's clean reservoir.
Each layer is assigned a target positional identity corresponding to a specific anatomical sub-region of the target organ. This assignment is registered in the build file at bioreactor configuration time. During the conditioning phase, the medium delivered to each layer is switched to a layer-specific cocktail containing morphogens, cytokines, and small molecules selected to drive cells in that layer toward the epigenetic state of the assigned sub-region. Signaling factors relevant to positional identity include, but are not limited to, retinoic acid at defined gradient concentrations establishing proximal-distal axis identity, Wnt pathway agonists and antagonists establishing zonation identity within organs such as the liver, BMP and FGF ligands at defined concentrations establishing dorsal-ventral and other positional axes, and additional organ-specific patterning signals as determined for each target tissue type.
The duration of the conditioning phase is determined by the target cell type and positional identity. Chromatin remodeling and stable epigenetic state transitions require sustained signaling over hours to days. The bioreactor dwell time accommodates this requirement as a terminal portion of the existing culture period, using modified medium composition rather than a separate vessel or process step.
Because each layer's positional identity assignment is registered in the build file, harvest routing is coordinated such that cells from each layer are delivered exclusively to the assembly positions anatomically concordant with that layer's assigned identity. A cell conditioned for periportal identity goes to a periportal assembly position. A cell conditioned for centrilobular identity goes to a centrilobular assembly position. The physical placement in the assembled construct is therefore concordant with the cell's internal positional epigenetic state. This concordance is established before assembly begins and is enforced by the routing architecture throughout.
A single bioreactor vessel can simultaneously condition cells to two or more distinct positional identities in independent layers, enabling a single build cycle to produce the full complement of sub-regional cell populations required for one organ without requiring multiple bioreactor units.
Following assembly, the perfusion awakening phase described in co-pending applications P3A and P3B reinforces and completes the cells' positional identity by delivering patterning signals through the vascular network at concentrations establishing spatial gradients concordant with the assembled anatomy. Bioreactor conditioning and perfusion awakening act as complementary stages of a two-stage positional identity system: conditioning establishes the initial epigenetic state before assembly, and perfusion awakening allows cells to integrate and finalize that state within the assembled tissue architecture.
4.18 Controlled-Environment Factor Reservoir System


Morphogenic and signaling factors used in positional identity conditioning, as well as specialty growth factors, cytokines, and small molecules used across all bioreactor phases, are chemically sensitive. Many degrade at culture temperatures, are damaged by oxygen or light exposure, and require specific storage conditions to maintain biological activity. Delivery of degraded or inactivated factors produces inconsistent or absent conditioning responses.
A simpler approach would pre-mix all required factors into a single conditioning medium and deliver the mixture through the layer clean reservoir. This approach is rejected because many biological factors are incompatible with each other at concentrated stock levels. When factors are combined as concentrates before dilution into a large aqueous volume, they encounter each other at concentrations orders of magnitude above their working levels. At these concentrations, protein-protein interactions, competitive binding, charge interactions, and direct biochemical antagonism between signaling molecules produce outcomes that range from partial inactivation to complete mutual cancellation to unpredictable off-target reactions. Two factors that work synergistically at correct working concentrations can partially or completely cancel each other when combined as concentrated stocks.
This phenomenon is well established across biological systems requiring multi-factor nutritional and signaling management, including plant tissue culture, hydroponic and aquaponic nutrient delivery, and fermentation process control. In each of these domains, practitioners discovered that concentrated solutions that appear chemically compatible on paper produce precipitation, antagonism, chelation, or degradation when combined before dilution. The standard solution across all of these fields is to maintain incompatible components as separate concentrated stocks and combine them only at the point of final dilution, where the large aqueous volume immediately disperses the molecules to working concentrations before significant interaction can occur. The present system applies this principle directly.
Each factor or validated compatible factor group is maintained as a separate concentrated stock in its own dedicated controlled-environment factor reservoir. Each factor reservoir is a sealed vessel providing independent control of the following environmental parameters:
Temperature. Each reservoir maintains its factor stock at a temperature selected for that factor's stability, ranging from cryogenic storage temperatures for the most sensitive materials down to refrigerated working stock temperatures. Temperature control is achieved by resistive heating elements and thermoelectric cooling elements integrated into the reservoir body with closed-loop feedback from a local temperature sensor.
Atmosphere. Factors sensitive to oxidative degradation are maintained under inert gas atmosphere within the reservoir headspace. Atmosphere composition is maintained by sealed reservoir construction with gas exchange ports controlled by miniature solenoid valves.
Light exclusion. Reservoirs containing photosensitive factors are constructed with opaque walls or housed within light-excluding enclosures. The factor solution is not exposed to ambient light at any point in the storage or delivery path.
Acoustic agitation. Some factor concentrates require gentle agitation to prevent aggregation or sedimentation. Low-amplitude acoustic agitation from PMUT elements integrated into the reservoir wall maintains homogeneous factor distribution without mechanical stirring and without shear-induced damage to protein structures.
Factors are delivered from their controlled-environment reservoirs to target layer clean reservoirs through dedicated microfluidic channels in the bioreactor manifold. Delivery is triggered by the layer controller specifying factor identity, quantity, and target layer. Each delivery event transfers a metered bolus of factor concentrate into the target clean reservoir volume, where it disperses to working concentration before the next factor arrives. Factors therefore meet each other only in dilute solution in the large reservoir volume — the condition under which their biological activities were characterized and validated — not as concentrates.
Delivery volume precision is achieved by acoustic micropumping. PMUT elements lining the delivery channel generate calibrated acoustic streaming pulses that advance a defined fluid volume per pulse. Pulse count determines delivered volume. This approach requires no mechanical valves or pumps in the delivery path, consistent with the all-acoustic-drive architecture of the base bioreactor system.
Because factors are maintained as separate stocks, the concentration of any individual factor can be adjusted independently without reformulating or revalidating a master mix. This provides iterative optimization capability that a pre-mixed approach cannot offer.
Each delivery event is logged with timestamp, factor identity, target layer, and delivered volume. This log constitutes the complete factor exposure record for every cell in every layer and forms part of the quality record for the assembled organ. The system verifies that each layer received its complete specified conditioning sequence before releasing cells for harvest.
Reservoir fill level is monitored by acoustic sensing of the liquid-headspace interface within each reservoir. Depletion alerts are issued to the build controller in sufficient advance to permit replenishment scheduling without interrupting a build cycle. Reservoirs are installed into the manifold bus by docking to standardized connectors that establish thermal, electrical, and fluidic connections simultaneously.
The controlled-environment factor reservoir system also supplies patterning signals to the perfusion awakening system during the post-assembly awakening phase, maintaining factor integrity through the entire organ production timeline from bioreactor conditioning through final vascular perfusion.
V. ADVANTAGES OVER PRIOR ART
Per-bead autonomous control. Every bead position in the bioreactor is an independently monitored, independently fed, independently managed unit. No prior art bioreactor provides individual electronic flow control, acoustic monitoring, and viral detection at every single cell growth position.
Exact real-time cell inventory. Every mitosis event, every cell death, and every free-floating cell in the system is detected and counted. The system controller maintains an exact—not estimated—inventory of every living cell at all times. No prior art cell manufacturing system provides single-cell-resolution real-time inventory across the full production population.
Per-bead pathogen isolation by through-flow. The vertical through-flow from clean reservoir through the bead position to dirty reservoir isolates each bead from its neighbors with Péclet numbers exceeding 1,000 for virus-sized particles. Pathogen containment is enforced by physics at every bead position without antivirals, immune cells, or biological intervention.
Provocation quarantine with per-bead verification. Deliberate chemical provocation of latent viral reactivation, combined with per-bead flow isolation and per-bead viral sentinel detection, provides active pathogen clearance verified at the individual bead level. No prior art combines provocation, isolation, and per-bead sentinel verification in a single integrated system.
Seamless quarantine-to-production transition. The same hardware performs quarantine and production growth without cell transfer, vessel change, or reconfiguration. Verified-clean cells continue growing in place.
Zero pressure, zero valves, zero pumps. All fluid motion is PMUT acoustic streaming with electronic coordination. No mechanical fluid handling components exist in the system except in the bead coating system. Per-bead flow control eliminates the position-dependent flow gradients inherent to pressure-driven plenum distribution.
Autonomous disruption recovery. Physical disruption events are handled gracefully by local per-bead control units without external intervention, with automatic bead relocation, dead cell flushing, loose cell capture, and exact post-disruption inventory.
Scalable rectangular geometry. The sandwich-stack slab scales independently in three dimensions: column length, column count, and layer count. Capacity is configured by slab dimensions to match any production requirement.
Zero-consumable sterilization.
Defense in depth pathogen management. Five independent barriers operate in series, any one of which is sufficient to prevent pathogen transmission: per-bead through-flow isolation that sweeps contaminants to the drain before diffusion can carry them to a neighbor; per-bead viral sentinel sensors detecting virion mass on concentrated single-bead effluent; per-bead acoustic detection of cytopathic effect signatures in individual cells; provocation quarantine that forces latent viruses to reveal themselves and triggers localized cavitation sterilization of affected positions; and reconditioning loop nanofiltration and UV-C irradiation that decontaminates all spent medium before it returns to the clean reservoir.

ABSTRACT
A rectangular sandwich-stack bioreactor for mammalian cell culture in microgravity comprising repeating layers, each layer having a clean nutrient reservoir with agitator PMUTs, a bead layer in which chemically tempered glass microcarrier beads are held and oriented by dedicated PMUT elements adjusting acoustic radiation pressure in real time, and a dirty effluent reservoir with sweeper PMUTs. Each bead position has an autonomous PMUT control unit that pulls fresh medium from the clean reservoir, pushes spent medium to the dirty reservoir, monitors every cell detecting each mitosis and death event, and measures viral sentinel resonant frequency on concentrated single-bead effluent. All fluid motion is PMUT acoustic streaming with no pumps, valves, or pressure. Per-bead through-flow provides pathogen isolation with Péclet numbers exceeding 1,000. A provocation quarantine protocol forces latent viral reactivation while per-bead isolation contains infected cells, sentinels detect events, and affected positions are cavitation-sterilized and reseeded from clean neighbors. The system maintains exact real-time inventory of every living cell. The system further provides a positional identity conditioning capability wherein each bioreactor layer is independently assigned a target anatomical sub-region identity and receives a layer-specific morphogen and signaling factor cocktail during a conditioning phase preceding harvest, coordinated with harvest routing to ensure each cell is placed in an assembly position concordant with its conditioned epigenetic state; dedicated controlled-environment factor reservoirs maintain each factor under independent temperature, atmosphere, and light conditions and deliver factors as separate boluses into the large clean reservoir volume to prevent factor-factor incompatibility reactions that occur when concentrated stocks are pre-mixed prior to dilution.
