PATENT APPLICATION 
Non-Provisional PATENT APPLICATION
UNIFIED STEREOTACTIC ASSEMBLY, INCUBATION, AND TRANSPORT SYSTEM FOR BIOLOGICAL TISSUE, ORGAN, AND LIMB MANUFACTURING
 Inventor: James C. Brake  |  February 2026
1. FIELD OF THE INVENTION
This invention relates to a unified stereotactic frame that serves without modification as the assembly chamber, incubation vessel, maturation environment, shipping container, and surgical delivery system for biological tissues, organs, and limbs manufactured in microgravity. The invention eliminates all inter-vessel transfers between manufacture and implantation, reducing handling-induced damage to zero for all phases following initial assembly completion.


2. BACKGROUND
Prior art in tissue engineering universally requires transfer of manufactured tissue between discrete vessels at multiple lifecycle stages: from assembly chamber to incubator, from incubator to shipping container, from shipping container to surgical preparation vessel, and finally to the operating field. Each transfer introduces mechanical stress, contamination risk, thermal disruption, and connection breaks in perfusion continuity. For vascularized tissue operating under continuous perfusion, any interruption in flow risks ischemic cascade and irreversible cellular damage.


Additionally, prior art assembly chambers are generic in geometry, requiring the manufactured tissue to conform to standardized mold shapes rather than patient-specific anatomy. Surgical anastomosis is therefore performed without pre-registered vascular, lymphatic, or neural connection points, requiring intraoperative improvisation for connection alignment.


No prior art system maintains a tissue construct within a single continuous vessel from the moment of assembly through delivery to the operating room, with integrated perfusion support, patient-specific interface geometry, and neural conditioning capability throughout the entire duration.


3. SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION
The present invention is a Unified Stereotactic Assembly, Incubation, and Transport System (USAITS), hereinafter referred to as the Stereotactic Frame (STF), which is a single patient-specific vessel that performs all of the following functions in sequence without modification or tissue transfer:


(a) Structural scaffold for deposition unit (DU) positioning during acoustic cell assembly in microgravity;
(b) Sealed incubation chamber following DU withdrawal and port plugging;
(c) Maturation and conditioning environment during orbital and transit phases;
(d) Pressure-equalized shipping vessel during atmospheric reentry and ground transport;
(e) Surgical delivery system arriving at the operating room with tissue at physiological temperature under continuous perfusion.


The STF is manufactured by additive fabrication (3D printing) from patient imaging data and encodes the patient's specific vascular inlet and outlet topology, lymphatic drainage geometry, and neural interface pad positions directly into its structure. The STF is a single-use disposable device, patient-locked from fabrication through implantation.


4. DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF THE INVENTION
4.1  Stereotactic Frame Architecture
The STF is an additively fabricated enclosure whose interior geometry precisely mirrors the target tissue, organ, or limb as determined from the patient's pre-operative imaging data. The exterior geometry is standardized for interfacing with orbital handling systems, perfusion machine attachment points, and ground transport fixtures.


The STF material is a biocompatible polymer or composite selected for: compatibility with continuous immersion in perfusion fluid at physiological temperatures over durations ranging from several days to several weeks; structural integrity sufficient to withstand handling forces and atmospheric pressure differentials during transport; compatibility with standard autoclave or gamma sterilization protocols prior to use; and additive fabrication precision sufficient to hold dimensional tolerances required for patient-specific interface geometry.


4.2  Deposition Unit Port Array
The STF incorporates a plurality of through-ports distributed across its surface geometry. Each port is sized and positioned to accept a specific deposition unit (DU) for the duration of the assembly phase. Port positions are determined computationally from the assembly sequence plan for the specific tissue type and patient anatomy, ensuring each port provides a DU access vector to the tissue region requiring cell deposition.


Each port incorporates a fitting assembly comprising: an inner sleeve that constrains the DU during assembly and provides a sealing surface upon DU withdrawal; a locking mechanism that secures the DU against vibration or fluid pressure during operation; and a valve seat sized to accept a corresponding plug assembly following DU withdrawal.


4.3  Port Plug System and Sealed Transition
Following completion of tissue assembly, all DUs are withdrawn from the STF in a coordinated sequence managed by the same acoustic navigation system used for DU placement. The navigation system retains the precise three-dimensional coordinates and approach vector for each port from the assembly phase and uses this data to guide plug insertion without requiring re-registration.


Each plug is fabricated from cell-safe biocompatible material and incorporates one or more silicone elastomer O-ring seals sized to the port inner sleeve dimension. Plug insertion is accomplished by a single axial push along the port approach vector, with the O-ring seals providing both fluid-tight sealing and mechanical retention. The plug design is configured to withstand internal fluid pressure consistent with the operating perfusion pressure plus safety margin, and to withstand handling and transport shock loads without seal failure.


Upon completion of plug insertion across all ports, the STF transitions from assembly chamber to sealed incubation vessel. No tissue transfer occurs. The tissue construct remains in the identical fluid environment, at the identical temperature, under identical perfusion conditions that existed during the final assembly phase. This transition is the critical departure from all prior art systems.


4.4  Patient-Specific Vascular, Lymphatic, and Neural Interface
The STF incorporates a fixed set of primary connection ports whose positions, orientations, and internal geometries are derived from the specific patient's anatomy as captured in pre-operative imaging. These patient-specific interface elements include:


Vascular inlet and outlet connections, positioned and sized to correspond to the patient's specific arterial supply and venous drainage anatomy at the intended implantation site. The connection geometry is designed to minimize anastomosis complexity in the operating room by pre-registering the tissue's major vessels to the patient's receiving anatomy.


Lymphatic drainage connections positioned to correspond to the patient's regional lymphatic architecture, enabling pre-registration of lymphatic continuity at implantation.


Neural interface pad positions corresponding to the patient's peripheral nerve fascicle topology at the implantation site, as captured by pre-operative nerve imaging modalities including but not limited to MRI neurography and intraoperative nerve mapping data incorporated into the STF fabrication dataset.


Extended vascular, lymphatic, and neural fiber lengths are printed with deliberate excess beyond the minimum required for implantation. This excess provides surgeons with margin for final anatomical adjustment, trimming, and custom anastomosis positioning at the operating field. For limb constructs, the entire limb is printed complete and the surgeon trims to the patient's specific amputation anatomy intraoperatively.


4.5  Neural Conditioning Interface
The STF incorporates a multi-electrode array (MEA) embedded at positions corresponding to the patient's peripheral nerve fascicle locations. The MEA architecture is adapted from organoid computing and neural interface platforms and provides both stimulation and recording capability at each electrode position.


During the incubation and transit phases, the MEA delivers stimulation patterns derived from the patient's pre-operative electromyographic and nerve conduction data, conditioning the printed neural tissue to firing signatures compatible with the patient's existing nervous system. Recording capability provides continuous confirmation that neural tissue is responding to stimulation, providing quality verification data throughout transit.


MEA power and signal management is provided by the attached perfusion and conditioning machine described in Section 4.7.


4.6  Thermal Management and Cellular Temperature Protocol
Cells exist at reduced temperature (approximately 4 degrees Celsius) only during the brief transit between the orbital bioreactor system and the assembly chamber inlet. This dormancy phase is limited to the minimum duration required for acoustic cell transport through the conveyor system to the DU barrel and tissue surface.


Immediately following placement at the tissue surface, cells transition to assembly temperature. Following completion of assembly and initiation of perfusion awakening, the tissue construct is brought to and maintained at physiological operating temperature for the remainder of the STF lifecycle, including the entire incubation, maturation, transit, and surgical delivery phases.


The STF incorporates thermal management features including insulating wall construction and compatibility with external thermal regulation provided by the attached perfusion machine. The goal is maintenance of a stable physiological temperature environment from awakening through OR delivery without thermal cycling.


4.7  Attached Perfusion and Conditioning Machine
A mobile perfusion, oxygenation, and nutrient delivery machine is attached to the STF from the initiation of perfusion awakening and remains attached and operational through all subsequent phases including incubation, orbital maturation, transit, and surgical delivery. The machine connects to the STF through dedicated perfusion ports separate from the DU port array.


The perfusion machine provides: continuous circulation of oxygenated, nutrient-supplemented perfusion fluid through the tissue vascular network; waste product removal; thermal regulation of the perfusion fluid and by conduction the tissue construct; power supply and signal management for the MEA neural conditioning array; and real-time monitoring of tissue health parameters including oxygenation, metabolic activity, and neural response.


The perfusion machine is designed for continuous operation through orbital handling, atmospheric reentry, ground transport, and operating room entry. The machine and STF together constitute the complete tissue delivery system that arrives at the operating room as a unit.


4.8  Pressure Equalization During Transport
The STF interior is maintained at a pressure substantially equal to the exterior ambient pressure during all transport phases. This pressure equalization eliminates net force across the STF walls and seals, reducing seal stress and leakage risk to near zero. The primary function of the sealed STF during transport is therefore containment of the perfusion fluid within the individual STF unit rather than structural pressure vessel function.


Where multiple STF units are transported simultaneously, each unit operates as an independent sealed environment at the same temperature and pressure as adjacent units. Units do not share fluid between them.


4.9  Orbital Maturation Protocol for Transport Survivability
Orbital maturation of tissue constructs within the STF is conducted to the minimum degree necessary to achieve transport survivability, not to achieve full functional conditioning. Full functional maturation, including complete Wolff's Law bone remodeling for limb constructs, completion of vascular compliance training, and full neural integration, is completed in Earth-based facilities following delivery.


This protocol division maximizes orbital manufacturing capacity by minimizing per-unit orbital occupancy time, allowing more units to complete assembly and achieve transport readiness within a given orbital manufacturing cycle.


Earth-based completion of maturation and conditioning utilizes Earth-gravity loading environments, patient-specific rehabilitation protocols, full clinical monitoring infrastructure, and direct integration with the patient's own physiological parameters including body weight, gait, and neural signals.


4.10  Operating Room Delivery and Surgical Interface
The STF and attached perfusion machine arrive at the operating room with the tissue construct at physiological temperature under continuous perfusion. The STF remains sealed and the tissue remains unpacked until the surgical team is prepared for implantation.


Unpacking and tissue handling are conducted in a controlled aqueous environment at physiological temperature. The patient-specific vascular, lymphatic, and neural interface geometries pre-registered in the STF structure reduce intraoperative anastomosis complexity. Excess vascular, lymphatic, and neural extensions printed into the construct provide surgical margin for final adjustment.


In the event of any compromise to STF integrity, perfusion continuity, or tissue health monitoring during any phase from assembly through OR delivery, the affected STF unit is retired and the tissue construct is discarded. No compromised construct is implanted. The manufacturing system initiates reprint protocols for the affected patient.

6. ABSTRACT
A unified Stereotactic Frame (STF) is disclosed that serves without modification as the assembly chamber, incubation vessel, maturation environment, shipping container, and surgical delivery system for biological tissues, organs, and limbs manufactured in orbital microgravity. The STF is additively fabricated from patient imaging data with interior geometry mirroring the target anatomy. A plurality of through-ports accept deposition units during acoustic cell assembly; following assembly, deposition units are withdrawn and ports sealed with silicone O-ring plugs using the same navigation system used for placement. Patient-specific vascular, lymphatic, and neural connection ports are pre-registered to the patient's implantation anatomy. An embedded multi-electrode array provides neural conditioning during transit using patient-derived stimulation patterns. A perfusion machine attaches at awakening and remains attached through operating room delivery. Biological cells exist at dormancy temperature only during bioreactor-to-assembler transit; physiological temperature is maintained from awakening through implantation without thermal cycling. Orbital maturation is limited to transport survivability; full functional maturation completes on Earth. No inter-vessel tissue transfer occurs at any point between assembly completion and surgical delivery. The STF and attached perfusion machine constitute the complete delivery system arriving at the operating room as a unit.
