AND TRIAGE-COORDINATED DELIVERY TO CELL CULTURE SYSTEMS

I. FIELD OF THE INVENTION
The present invention relates to automated cell processing systems for microgravity environments. More specifically, the invention provides a miniaturized, autonomous warming and reanimation machine (WRM) that receives hypothermically preserved patient-derived cells from a standardized syringe interface, warms them at cell-type-specific controlled rates, performs acoustic and optical viability assessment on each individual cell, executes a triage protocol coordinated with the build file governing organ assembly, and delivers viable cells in acoustically managed medium slugs to downstream cell culture systems. The WRM is designed as a small, self-contained unit deployable in large parallel arrays on a manifold bus aboard a microgravity bio-manufacturing facility.


II. BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION
The Hypothermic Preservation Transition Problem
Patient-derived cells for organ manufacturing are preserved and transported at hypothermic temperatures, typically 2 to 8 degrees Celsius, to suppress metabolic activity and extend viable shelf life without the physical damage associated with cryopreservation. At these temperatures, cellular metabolism slows to approximately 10 to 15 percent of its normal rate. Ion pumps slow, sodium leaks into the cell, potassium leaks out, and a metabolic debt accumulates over time. The cell remains structurally intact but carries a burden of ion imbalance, mild lactate accumulation, and oxidative stress products that must be resolved rapidly upon rewarming.
The transition from hypothermic preservation to active culture is a period of significant cellular vulnerability. The rate of warming, the composition of the medium present during warming, and the availability of oxygen and glucose in the first minutes following temperature recovery all determine whether the cell successfully re-establishes ion homeostasis and resumes normal function or undergoes irreversible injury. Cells that survive the transition with full functional capacity, cells that survive but are stressed and will die within hours of entering culture, and cells that do not survive the transition at all must be reliably distinguished before entering the culture system, because placing non-viable or severely stressed cells on culture substrate wastes capacity and introduces debris and inflammatory signals that adversely affect neighboring viable cells.
Limitations of Existing Cell Processing Approaches
Existing approaches to post-thaw or post-hypothermic cell processing are inadequate for the requirements of large-scale automated organ manufacturing in microgravity. Manual microscopy and trypan blue exclusion assays provide population-level viability estimates but cannot assess individual cells prior to seeding, rely on membrane-impermeant dyes incompatible with subsequent culture, and require human operator involvement incompatible with fully automated orbital manufacturing. Flow cytometry provides high-throughput single-cell analysis but processes cells in one orientation at high velocity, precluding full three-dimensional morphological assessment, and routes all cells through a single serial processing path incompatible with the parallel, coordinated multi-cell-type scheduling required for organ builds.
Furthermore, no existing system provides the coordinated triage logic required for organ manufacturing: the ability to compare available cell counts against required build counts per cell type, compute whether the available viable population is sufficient to meet harvest targets given expected growth rates, calculate the impact of shortfalls on build schedule, and make autonomous routing decisions — routing cells to culture, holding reserve syringes, or escalating to ground control — without human intervention.
Need for the Present Invention
What is needed is a small, autonomous, parallel-deployable cell processing unit that performs the complete cell intake, warming, viability assessment, triage, and delivery sequence for a single patient cell type from a single syringe, coordinates with the organ build file governing the manufacturing process, and operates without human intervention in a microgravity environment. Deploying thousands of such units in parallel on a manifold bus enables simultaneous processing of all cell types required for an organ build on the coordinated schedule specified by the build file.


III. SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION
The present invention provides a warming and reanimation machine (WRM) comprising a small sealed processing chamber lined with piezoelectric micromachined ultrasonic transducer (PMUT) arrays and equipped with an integrated optical microscopy system, a standardized syringe docking interface, a controlled-environment factor reservoir system, and a manifold output interface. The WRM receives hypothermically preserved cells from a patient package syringe, warms them at a controlled cell-type-specific rate specified by the build file, performs continuous acoustic monitoring and optical inspection of each individual cell during and after warming, classifies each cell into one of four viability categories, executes a triage protocol comparing available viable cell counts against build requirements, and delivers viable cells to downstream culture systems in acoustically managed medium slugs via the whisper-power PMUT transport architecture.
Multiple WRM units operate simultaneously in parallel arrays on the bio-manufacturing facility manifold bus, with each unit processing one syringe of one cell type. The build file coordinates activation timing across all WRM units such that cell types with similar maturation timelines enter culture simultaneously and all cell types reach peak culture condition on the coordinated harvest schedule.


IV. DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF THE INVENTION
4.1 Physical Architecture of the WRM
The WRM is a small sealed vessel of rectangular or cylindrical geometry, sized to accommodate the cell count delivered by a standard patient package syringe. The interior surfaces of the vessel carry dense arrays of PMUT elements providing acoustic manipulation, acoustic monitoring, and acoustic streaming fluid management throughout the chamber volume, in the same architectural principle as the sandwich-stack bioreactor described in co-pending applications. The WRM contains no mechanical pumps, no mechanical valves, and no pressure sources. All fluid motion within the chamber is generated by PMUT acoustic streaming.
The chamber contains an array of acoustic well positions, each configured to hold one individual cell at a defined location within the chamber volume. An acoustic well is a stable pressure node generated by the PMUT array that holds a single cell in place against fluid currents and thermal convection. The number of acoustic well positions is sized to the expected maximum cell count from the syringe volume for the cell type being processed. Well positions are arranged in a regular grid to maximize acoustic access from surrounding PMUT elements and to enable systematic optical scanning by the integrated microscopy system.
The chamber wall carries an integrated optical microscopy system comprising one or more miniaturized imaging elements providing brightfield illumination and image capture of the well array. The optical system is configured to image individual cells at sufficient magnification to resolve membrane morphology, cytoplasmic granularity, and nuclear structure. Because the PMUT array can rotate cells in place by applying asymmetric acoustic field patterns, the optical system acquires multi-angle images of each cell without requiring physical movement of the imaging element.
4.2 Syringe Interface
The WRM syringe interface is a standardized docking port accepting patient package syringes of 3 millimeters inner diameter with a twist-off O-ring sealed cap. The syringe contains patient-derived cells in liquid suspension at hypothermic preservation temperature. The interior surface of the syringe carries anti-fouling treatment to prevent cell adhesion to the syringe wall during transport and storage.
Before the syringe plunger is activated, the WRM chamber is brought to thermal equilibrium with the syringe contents. This prevents thermal shock to cells at the inlet as they transition from the syringe to the chamber environment. Temperature equilibration is monitored by thermal sensors in the chamber wall and the docking interface. The plunger is activated by the WRM controller at a cell-type-specific rate specified in the build file, controlling the fluid velocity and shear stress experienced by cells as they enter the chamber. Fragile cell types such as neurons and hepatocytes receive slower plunge rates than more mechanically robust cell types.
4.3 Cell Reception and Initial Distribution
As cells enter the chamber from the syringe, the PMUT array activates in reception mode. Incoming cells are detected acoustically as they cross the inlet zone. Clumps of cells — aggregates formed during preservation or transport — are detected by their anomalously large acoustic cross-section and are subjected to acoustic separation: calibrated low-amplitude ultrasonic pulses that progressively dissociate cell-cell contacts without membrane damage. Separation is confirmed when the acoustic signature resolves to single-cell dimensions.
Individual cells are directed to vacant acoustic well positions by acoustic streaming currents generated by the PMUT array. Each well's PMUT elements activate to capture an arriving cell and stabilize it at the well position. The WRM controller maintains a real-time map of well occupancy across the entire chamber. Cells that cannot be directed to a well because all positions are occupied are held in a buffer zone at the inlet end of the chamber until capacity becomes available.
4.4 Pre-Warming Baseline Monitoring — Step 4
With cells distributed to well positions and chamber temperature still matching the syringe preservation temperature, the WRM performs a baseline acoustic characterization of every cell simultaneously. Each well's PMUT elements execute a pulse-echo measurement sequence generating acoustic impedance, cell diameter, and membrane mechanical response data for the cell at that position. This baseline characterization establishes a reference state for each individual cell against which subsequent warming-phase measurements are compared.
Dead or grossly compromised cells are often identifiable at this baseline stage. Cells with severely disrupted membranes show anomalous acoustic impedance. Cells that lysed during transport show fragmented or diffuse acoustic signatures. Cells that aggregated and could not be fully separated are flagged for continued monitoring. All baseline data is logged per well position and transmitted to the build controller.
4.5 Controlled Warming and Reanimation Monitoring — Step 5
The WRM initiates controlled warming of the chamber contents at a rate specified per cell type in the build file. Warming rate is controlled by resistive heating elements in the chamber walls with closed-loop feedback from distributed temperature sensors. The warming rate is calibrated to minimize thermal gradients within the chamber while avoiding the zone between approximately 15 and 30 degrees Celsius where partial metabolic activity combined with insufficient ion pump function creates peak cellular stress.
During warming, every well position undergoes continuous acoustic monitoring. The PMUT elements at each position execute repeated pulse-echo sequences at intervals of several seconds throughout the warming curve. The acoustic data stream for each cell is analyzed in real time for the characteristic metabolic reactivation signature: a progressive change in membrane mechanical response, cytoplasmic viscosity, and acoustic impedance that occurs as ion pumps resume activity, mitochondrial metabolism re-establishes, and the cell re-inflates to normal volume. This signature is distinct from the acoustic evolution of a non-viable cell, which shows no such organized transition during warming.
Simultaneously, the integrated optical system performs repeated imaging passes across the well array. At defined temperature intervals during warming, each cell is optically imaged in at least two orientations achieved by acoustic rotation. Morphological parameters including membrane smoothness, cytoplasmic homogeneity, and nuclear visibility are extracted from the optical images. Cells showing membrane blebbing, cytoplasmic vacuolation, or nuclear abnormality are flagged regardless of their acoustic signature.
The WRM's onboard control system integrates acoustic and optical data streams per well position throughout the warming sequence. A confidence score for cell viability is computed continuously for each cell, updated with each new measurement. The score incorporates acoustic transition dynamics, optical morphology at the end of warming, and baseline anomaly flags.
4.6 Viability Classification and First Sort — Step 6
Upon completion of warming to physiological temperature, the WRM classifies each cell into one of four categories based on its integrated acoustic and optical data record:
Strong. The cell showed a clear, early acoustic reactivation transition, normal morphology at all optical inspection points, and acoustic impedance within the expected range for the cell type. The cell is expected to attach and proliferate normally on culture substrate.
Good. The cell showed an acoustic reactivation transition within acceptable parameters, with minor anomalies that fall within tolerated variation. Morphology is acceptable. The cell is expected to perform normally in culture.
Weak. The cell showed a delayed, attenuated, or irregular acoustic reactivation signature, or showed mild morphological stress signs at optical inspection, or both. The cell is viable but stressed. It is expected to have higher dropout probability in early culture. It is routed to culture with a stress flag attached to its routing metadata, alerting the receiving culture system to apply enhanced monitoring at the receiving bead position.
Dead. The cell showed no acoustic reactivation transition, confirmed membrane compromise on optical inspection, or gross morphological abnormality. The cell is non-viable. It is routed to the waste stream and not delivered to culture.
4.7 Triage Protocol — Build File Coordination
Following classification, the WRM executes a triage protocol by querying the build file for the following parameters for this cell type: required seed count, which is the number of viable cells needed in culture to reach the target harvest count given the expected growth rate and projected dropout by viability category; strong and good count available, which is the count of cells classified Strong or Good from the current syringe; weak count available; and schedule buffer, which is the time margin available before this cell type becomes the critical path constraint for the build.
The triage decision logic proceeds as follows. If the Strong and Good count alone meets or exceeds the required seed count, Weak cells are routed to waste and the build proceeds without them. Using only the highest-confidence cells when supply is sufficient maximizes culture system efficiency and reduces monitoring load.
If the Strong and Good count falls short of the required seed count but the addition of Weak cells meets it, all viable cells including Weak are routed to culture. The build controller is notified that Weak cells have been included and expected dropout rates are adjusted in the build schedule accordingly.
If the total viable count including Weak cells falls short of the required seed count, the triage protocol evaluates whether the shortfall can be compensated by extended culture time within the available schedule buffer. If the schedule permits, the build controller recalculates the growth phase duration and reports the revised estimated completion time. If the patient package contains a reserve syringe for this cell type, the WRM requests activation of that syringe and its processing is initiated immediately.
If after processing all available syringes for this cell type the viable count remains critically insufficient and schedule compensation is not feasible, the build controller escalates to ground control with a status report specifying the shortfall, the affected cell type, the impact on build feasibility, and the options available. The escalation includes a recommendation based on which cell type is deficient — a shortfall in endothelial cells has different build feasibility implications than a shortfall in a minor stromal population — enabling informed ground decision-making.
If viable cell count is so low that no feasible build path exists for this patient, the build is aborted cleanly and ground control is notified. No cells are wasted on an infeasible build.
4.8 Cell Delivery in Medium Slugs — Step 7
Viable cells cleared for delivery are transferred from their acoustic well positions to the WRM outlet interface via the whisper-power PMUT acoustic transport architecture. Each cell travels within a discrete bolus of its conditioning medium, referred to as a medium slug, that surrounds the cell throughout the transit path from the WRM outlet to the receiving bead position in the culture system.
The medium slug serves multiple functions. It prevents direct contact between the cell and the transit channel walls, eliminating wall shear stress on the cell membrane. It maintains the cell's local chemical environment — oxygen tension, nutrient concentration, pH, and dissolved signaling factors — during transit. It provides an acoustically distinct transport object that the PMUT array elements lining the transit path can track and position with higher reliability than a single cell in open flow. Slug volume is specified per cell type in the build file, calibrated to provide sufficient chemical buffering for the expected transit duration.
Cells classified as Weak carry a stress flag in their routing metadata that is transmitted with the slug to the receiving culture system. The receiving per-bead control unit reads the stress flag and applies enhanced acoustic monitoring protocols to that bead position during the early culture period.
Strong and Good cells are delivered first. Weak cells follow after all Strong and Good cells have been dispatched, ensuring that the highest-quality cells reach available bead positions under optimal early culture conditions.
4.9 Chamber Cleansing and Syringe Disconnection — Step 8
Following completion of cell delivery, the WRM chamber undergoes an acoustic cleansing sequence. PMUT elements generate focused cavitation to disrupt and detach any cellular debris or protein deposits from chamber surfaces. Sweeper PMUT elements generate acoustic streaming to carry dislodged material to the waste outlet. The chamber is flushed with fresh medium from the manifold supply. A final acoustic census confirms that no cells or debris remain in the well positions. The syringe is then disconnected from the docking interface by the facility robot arm and the syringe is disposed of or transferred to the waste stream.
Following cleansing, the WRM is available to accept a new syringe. The unit is self-sterilizing between syringe loads using the same zero-consumable sterilization approach as the culture system: focused cavitation, ultraviolet irradiation through the chamber optical elements, and thermal cycling. The WRM is a reusable permanent facility component consuming no expendable sterilization materials.
4.10 Parallel Array Operation and Build File Scheduling
Multiple WRM units are mounted to the bio-manufacturing facility manifold bus alongside culture system units. Each WRM unit operates autonomously and independently, processing one syringe at a time. The build file specifies an activation schedule for each WRM unit based on the maturation timeline of the cell type it is assigned to process. Cell types requiring longer culture periods are activated first; cell types requiring shorter culture periods are activated later, such that all cell types reach peak culture condition simultaneously on the target harvest date.
Cell types with similar activation times are processed simultaneously across multiple WRM units operating in parallel. The parallel architecture eliminates the processing bottleneck that would arise from serial processing of all cell types through a single unit. Build file scheduling coordinates activation times, triage thresholds, and delivery sequencing across all active WRM units to maintain synchronized progress toward the harvest target.
4.11 Factor Reservoirs in the WRM
The WRM incorporates controlled-environment factor reservoirs supplying cell-type-specific recovery medium components during warming and early reanimation. Recovery medium differs from growth medium in composition: it is formulated for the specific needs of the reactivation transition, providing higher glucose concentration for immediate ATP synthesis demand, antioxidant components to manage the oxidative burst that occurs on mitochondrial reactivation, ion concentrations calibrated to assist pump restoration, and specific trophic factors supporting the target cell type's survival during early recovery.
Growth-promoting factors and proliferative signals are withheld from the recovery medium and introduced only after the cell has successfully completed the reactivation transition, as evidenced by completion of the acoustic signature transition and optical morphology confirmation. Introducing proliferative signals to a cell still completing the reactivation transition is counterproductive and increases stress-related dropout.
Factor reservoirs in the WRM operate under the same controlled-environment principles as those in the culture system: independent temperature control per reservoir, inert atmosphere for oxygen-sensitive factors, light exclusion for photosensitive factors, and acoustic agitation to maintain homogeneous factor distribution. Factors are delivered by acoustic micropumping into the chamber medium with sub-microliter precision.


V. ADVANTAGES OVER PRIOR ART
Individual cell resolution. Every cell entering the WRM receives individual acoustic and optical characterization throughout the warming sequence. No population averaging. No sampling. Every cell gets a pass or fail decision based on its own complete measurement record.
Dye-free viability assessment. The acoustic metabolic reactivation signature and multi-angle optical morphology assessment together provide high-confidence viability determination without membrane-impermeant dyes, fluorescent labels, or any chemical that would compromise cell function or contaminate the culture system.
Three-dimensional optical inspection. Acoustic rotation of each cell in its well position enables optical imaging from multiple angles, providing full morphological assessment that flat-plane imaging cannot achieve.
Build-file-integrated triage. The WRM does not make viability decisions in isolation. It integrates its cell census with the build file's requirements, computing whether the available viable population is sufficient for the build and what the schedule implications of any shortfall are. This is a manufacturing process control decision, not just a quality check.
Parallel scalability. Because each WRM is small and self-contained, processing capacity scales linearly with the number of units on the manifold bus. No single large system creates a processing bottleneck. Thousands of WRM units can operate simultaneously, each processing one cell type, matching the parallel architecture of the culture system they serve.
Zero consumable sterilization. The WRM self-sterilizes between syringe loads using acoustic cavitation, UV irradiation through existing optical elements, and thermal cycling. No sterilization chemicals, no replacement filters, no consumable materials beyond the patient package syringe itself.
Medium slug delivery. Cells are delivered to culture in individual medium slugs that protect them from transit shear, maintain their chemical environment during delivery, and carry routing metadata including viability classification and stress flags to the receiving culture system.

ABSTRACT
A warming and reanimation machine (WRM) for microgravity cell processing comprising a sealed PMUT-lined chamber with acoustic well positions each holding one individual cell, an integrated optical microscopy system, a standardized hypothermic syringe docking interface, controlled-environment factor reservoirs, and a manifold output interface. The WRM receives hypothermically preserved patient-derived cells, equilibrates temperature before cell introduction, distributes cells to individual acoustic wells, performs baseline characterization at preservation temperature, warms cells at a controlled cell-type-specific rate while monitoring each cell's acoustic metabolic reactivation signature and acquiring multi-angle optical images by acoustic rotation, classifies each cell as Strong, Good, Weak, or Dead, executes a triage protocol integrating available viable cell counts with build file requirements to determine routing and escalation decisions, and delivers viable cells to culture in individual medium slugs via acoustic transport. Multiple WRM units operate in parallel arrays on a manufacturing facility manifold bus, coordinated by the organ build file to process all required cell types simultaneously on the schedule required for synchronized culture maturation. The WRM self-sterilizes between syringe cycles without consumable sterilization materials.
